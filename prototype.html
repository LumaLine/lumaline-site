The Science Behind the Prototype

Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect forms the foundation of solar panel technology. Photons strike the surface of a semiconductor, releasing electrons and generating electric current:

\[ E_k = hf - \phi \]
where:

  • \( E_k \): Kinetic energy of the emitted electron
  • \( h \): Planck’s constant
  • \( f \): Frequency of incident light
  • \( \phi \): Work function (energy required to release the electron)

Physics Equations Used

Several essential physics equations are applied in the prototype's design:

\[ P = V \times I \quad \text{(Electric Power)} \]
\[ F = ma \quad \text{(Newton's Second Law)} \]
\[ \tau = r \times F \quad \text{(Torque)} \]
\[ E = P \times t \quad \text{(Energy)} \]

To calculate solar energy production:

\[ E_{\text{solar}} = A \cdot r \cdot H \cdot PR \]
where:

  • \( A \): Area of the solar panel
  • \( r \): Efficiency of the panel
  • \( H \): Solar irradiance (sunlight power per unit area)
  • \( PR \): Performance ratio (accounts for system losses)

Calculus for Performance Optimization

Calculus helps us optimize power output:

\[ P(t) = V(t) \cdot I(t) \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{dP}{dt} = V(t) \cdot \frac{dI}{dt} + I(t) \cdot \frac{dV}{dt} \]
where:

  • \( V(t) \): Voltage over time
  • \( I(t) \): Current over time
  • \( \frac{dP}{dt} \): Rate of change of power

To minimize energy loss due to resistance: \[ P_{\text{loss}} = I^2 R \]

Total energy output over time: \[ E_{\text{total}} = \int_{0}^{T} P(t) \, dt \]
where:

  • \( T \): Total time period
  • \( P(t) \): Instantaneous power at time \( t \)

Scalability and Future Potential

This prototype demonstrates that solar energy can be practically applied to transportation systems. With increased scale and refinement, such technology can power larger electric trains for regional transit—contributing to carbon neutrality and sustainable development in line with Vision 2030 and the Saudi Green Initiative.
